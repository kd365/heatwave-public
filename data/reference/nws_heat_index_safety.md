# National Weather Service — Heat Index Safety Guide

Source: https://www.weather.gov/safety/heat-illness and https://www.weather.gov/ama/heatindex

## Heat Index Definition

The Heat Index is a measure of how hot it really feels when relative humidity is factored in with the actual air temperature. It represents the apparent temperature — what the temperature feels like to the human body when relative humidity is combined with the air temperature.

If you are exposed to direct sunlight, the heat index value can be increased by up to 15F.

## Heat Index Danger Classifications

| Classification | Heat Index Range | Health Effects |
|---|---|---|
| Caution | 80F - 90F | Fatigue possible with prolonged exposure and/or physical activity |
| Extreme Caution | 90F - 103F | Heat stroke, heat cramps, or heat exhaustion possible with prolonged exposure and/or physical activity |
| Danger | 103F - 124F | Heat cramps or heat exhaustion likely, and heat stroke possible with prolonged exposure and/or physical activity |
| Extreme Danger | 125F or higher | Heat stroke highly likely |

## NWS Heat Advisory Criteria

A Heat Advisory is issued when the heat index is expected to reach 100-104F for 2 or more hours. Criteria vary by local NWS office based on regional acclimatization.

## NWS Excessive Heat Warning Criteria

An Excessive Heat Warning is issued when the heat index is expected to reach 105F or higher for 2 or more hours. This is the most severe NWS heat alert.

NOTE: These thresholds vary by region. In the Desert Southwest, an Excessive Heat Warning may not be issued until the heat index reaches 115F, while in the Pacific Northwest it may be issued at lower thresholds due to lower public acclimatization.

## Heat-Related Illnesses

### Heat Cramps
Symptoms: Painful muscle cramps and spasms usually in legs and abdomen. Heavy sweating.
First Aid: Apply pressure or massage to relieve spasms; give sips of water unless nausea occurs. Seek immediate medical attention if cramps persist beyond 1 hour.

### Heat Exhaustion
Symptoms: Heavy sweating; weakness or tiredness; cool, pale, clammy skin; fast, weak pulse; muscle cramps; dizziness, nausea or vomiting; headache; fainting.
First Aid: Move to air-conditioned area, loosen clothing, apply cool wet cloths or cool bath, offer water sips. Seek immediate medical attention if vomiting occurs, symptoms worsen, or last over 1 hour.

### Heat Stroke
Symptoms: Body temperature above 103F; hot, red, dry or damp skin; rapid and strong pulse; fainting; loss of consciousness.
First Aid: Call 911 immediately — this is a severe medical emergency. Move to air-conditioned environment, apply cool cloths/bath. Do NOT give fluids.

## Important Warning About Fans

Fans may make conditions worse when heat index temperatures are above the 90s. At high heat index values, fans can increase body heat rather than cool.

## Conflict Note: Heat Index vs. WBGT

The NWS Heat Index system uses only two variables (temperature and relative humidity) to assess danger. In contrast, OSHA and NIOSH recommend using the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) which incorporates four factors: temperature, humidity, wind speed, and radiant heat. This can lead to conflicting risk assessments:

- A shaded area with wind may show a "Danger" Heat Index of 110F but a moderate WBGT of 28C (within safe limits for light work).
- A sunlit, windless area may show a "Extreme Caution" Heat Index of 95F but a WBGT of 33C (exceeding all safe work limits).

Emergency responders must understand which metric is being used when interpreting heat hazard data from different sources.
